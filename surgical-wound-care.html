Surgical Wound Care
Post-Op Dressing Strategy

Every surgical incision is a controlled wound — but how it heals depends on how it closes, how clean the operative field was, and what covers it for the critical 48 hours afterwards. Surgical site infection (SSI) remains the second most common healthcare-associated infection in high-income countries, with colorectal procedures showing rates near 9.5%. This guide aligns the CDC contamination classes and the three healing intentions (primary, secondary, tertiary) with the matching post-operative dressing strategy — built around WHO and NICE evidence-based recommendations.

Every Incision Heals
in Four Predictable Phases

Surgical wound healing follows a tightly orchestrated biological sequence: haemostasis, inflammation, proliferation, and remodelling. A well-chosen dressing supports each phase — protecting against contamination during the vulnerable inflammatory window, absorbing fluid during proliferation, and minimising scarring through remodelling. Get the dressing wrong, and any phase can stall or reverse.

The Four Phases of Surgical Wound Healing
⬡

Haemostasis · 0–6 Hours

The moment the scalpel makes the incision, vasoconstriction and platelet aggregation begin sealing the wound. A fibrin clot forms within minutes, creating the provisional matrix that all subsequent healing builds upon. This phase ends when bleeding stops — typically within the first hours after closure. The dressing's job here is mechanical: protect the clot, absorb residual oozing, and provide a sterile barrier against the operative environment.

◈

Inflammation · 6–72 Hours

Neutrophils flood the site within hours; macrophages dominate by day 2–3. Bacteria, debris and damaged tissue are cleared. This is the most vulnerable phase for surgical site infection — pathogens have a narrow window to colonise before host defences and an intact barrier dressing seal the wound. WHO and NICE specifically recommend leaving the primary dressing undisturbed for a minimum of 48 hours unless leakage or clinical deterioration demands otherwise.

◇

Proliferation · Day 3 to Day 21

Fibroblasts produce collagen, new capillaries form, and granulation tissue fills any defect. Epithelial cells migrate across the closed surface — in primary closure this is largely complete within 4–7 days; in secondary intention, it can take weeks. Dressings during this phase must balance moisture (too dry desiccates new tissue; too wet macerates the peri-wound skin) and protect mechanically fragile granulation tissue from disruption at every change.

◆

Remodelling · 3 Weeks to 2 Years

Type III collagen is progressively replaced by stronger Type I collagen; the scar contracts and matures. Final tensile strength reaches only 80% of unwounded skin. Patient factors — diabetes, smoking, malnutrition, corticosteroid use — slow every preceding phase and reduce final tensile strength. How the wound is dressed in the first 14 days strongly predicts the cosmetic and functional outcome two years later.

CDC Wound Classification & Healing Intention

Two Dimensions That
Define Every Surgical Wound

Surgical wounds are classified along two intersecting axes: the CDC contamination class (predicts infection risk) and the closure / healing intention (determines dressing strategy). Procurement and ward-level dressing protocols flow from this matrix.

CDC Contamination Classes

Class IClean. Elective, atraumatic, no inflammation. No entry into respiratory, alimentary, genital or urinary tracts. SSI rate <2%. Examples: knee replacement, breast biopsy, thyroidectomy.
Class IIClean-Contaminated. Controlled entry into respiratory, alimentary, genital or urinary tract without unusual contamination. SSI rate ~3–8%. Examples: elective colorectal, cholecystectomy, hysterectomy, C-section.
Class IIIContaminated. Open fresh accidental wounds, major breaks in sterile technique, or gross spillage from GI tract; acute non-purulent inflammation. SSI rate ~10–17%. Examples: penetrating trauma, open fractures.
Class IVDirty / Infected. Old traumatic wounds with retained devitalised tissue, existing clinical infection or perforated viscera. SSI rate >27%. Examples: faecal peritonitis, drained abscess.

Three Healing Intentions

PrimaryEdges approximated. Sutures, staples, glue or strips bring clean edges together; minimal tissue loss, predictable healing in 5–14 days. The dominant pattern for elective surgery.
SecondaryOpen, granulating from base. Cannot be closed primarily due to tissue loss, contamination or tension. Heals from the wound bed upward through granulation, contraction and re-epithelialisation. Weeks to months.
TertiaryDelayed primary closure. Wound left open initially (typically 3–7 days) to manage contamination or oedema, then closed surgically once clean. Combines features of both above.
Decision Dimensions

Five Variables That
Drive Dressing Choice

A structured framework procurement teams and surgical wards can use to standardise dressing selection. Every decision moves through these five questions in sequence.

C

Closure Method & Intention

Primary closure → low-adhesive interactive dressing for 48 hours minimum, then reassess. Secondary → moisture-balanced active dressing matched to exudate. Tertiary → contamination control until closure.

E

Exudate Volume

Dry / minimal → hydrocolloid or transparent film. Low–moderate → silicone-bordered foam. Moderate–heavy → silicone foam with high absorbency. Heavy with cavity → alginate primary + foam secondary.

I

Infection Risk & Status

High SSI risk (CDC II–IV, BMI >40, diabetes, immunosuppression) → silver-impregnated dressing prophylactically. Established infection → nano-silver antimicrobial; review every 7 days for de-escalation.

S

Skin Sensitivity at Site

Fragile peri-wound skin, elderly, oncology patients on corticosteroids → soft silicone contact layer or fully non-adhesive foam. Avoid acrylic adhesives that strip the stratum corneum on removal (MARSI risk).

L

Location & Movement

High-flexion areas (joints, abdomen) → flexible silicone foam with bordered edges. Visible cosmetic sites (face, neck) → ultra-thin hydrocolloid or transparent film for discreet, low-profile cover. Dependent areas → waterproof outer film.

Post-Operative Timeline

From Operating Theatre
to Final Closure

The dressing strategy evolves as the wound matures. Below is the standard post-operative timeline for a primary-closure incision — annotated with the dressing action at each transition. Wounds healing by secondary intention follow a parallel but extended trajectory.

1

Theatre · Initial Cover

Sterile, low-adhesive interactive dressing applied at end of procedure. WHO and NICE recommend an advanced dressing for higher-risk patients (CDC Class II+, obese, diabetic). The primary dressing is selected to remain in place for 48 hours minimum unless leakage or clinical deterioration demands removal.

2

0–48 Hours · Undisturbed

Inflammatory phase is at its peak. Do not disturb the primary dressing unless strike-through, lifting or clinical concern. Modern bordered silicone foam dressings provide waterproof protection — patients may shower from 48 hours in most protocols.

3

Day 2–5 · First Inspection

Remove primary dressing carefully (silicone borders > acrylic for atraumatic removal). Inspect for SSI signs: erythema beyond wound edge, warmth, induration, discharge, dehiscence. Replace with same category if healing on track; escalate to antimicrobial if early SSI signs.

4

Day 5–14 · Proliferation

Granulation and re-epithelialisation dominate. For primary closure, the wound is largely sealed by day 7; for secondary intention, granulation tissue fills the defect. Match dressing to exudate: foam for moderate, alginate for heavy or cavity, hydrocolloid for low. Sutures or staples typically removed at days 7–14 depending on site.

5

Week 3+ · Remodelling

Surface healing complete in primary closure; secondary-intention wounds still progressing. Scar maturation begins — soft, supportive dressings or silicone scar therapy can be introduced (see Scar Management). Patient discharged to home care or outpatient follow-up.

!

Complication Pathway

If SSI develops or wound dehisces: aggressive intervention required. Wound culture, debridement, antimicrobial dressing (nano-silver), and possible escalation to negative-pressure wound therapy for large defects. Documentation triggers infection-control review and surgical site infection surveillance reporting.

SSI Prevention · Evidence-Based Bundle

The Care Bundle
That Prevents SSI

Surgical site infection is largely preventable through a structured perioperative bundle. The 2018 WHO Global Guidelines and the 2024 NICE NG125 update converge on the same protocol: standardise the prep, choose the right closure technique, cover with an appropriate dressing, and resist disturbing it for 48 hours. Below is the operational version. Velcura's role sits at one specific layer of this bundle — the right post-operative dressing — but procurement decisions are best made with the full context in view.

Escalate clinically when
  • Erythema spreading more than 5 mm beyond the wound edge — early superficial SSI suspected
  • Increasing localised pain, warmth, induration or purulent discharge — established SSI
  • Wound dehiscence (partial or complete separation of edges) — surgical review required
  • Systemic signs: fever >38°C, raised WBC or CRP, tachycardia — possible deep incisional or organ/space SSI
  • Pre-operative skin preparation — chlorhexidine-alcohol > aqueous povidone-iodine for most surgeries (WHO strong recommendation, NICE concurs).
  • Surgical antibiotic prophylaxis — single dose within 60 minutes of incision; re-dose during long procedures or major blood loss. Avoid extending post-operatively without indication.
  • Maintain perioperative normothermia, normoglycaemia and tissue oxygenation — three modifiable factors with strong evidence in major surgery.
  • Choose the closure method by tissue plane — sutures, staples or skin glue each have specific indications; triclosan-coated sutures are recommended by WHO for reduction of SSI.
  • Cover with an interactive dressing matched to risk — silicone-bordered foam (with silver for higher-risk patients) for primary closure; advanced moisture-balanced dressings for secondary intention.
  • Leave the primary dressing undisturbed for at least 48 hours unless leakage, lifting or clinical deterioration. Post-operative care bundles consistently recommend this.
  • Educate the patient before discharge — when to shower, what SSI signs warrant urgent review, expected dressing change cadence. Patient self-monitoring is now part of best practice in same-day and short-stay surgery.
SSI Risk × CDC Class

The Surgical Wound
Dressing Pyramid

Dressing strategy scales with infection risk. This pyramid maps the CDC contamination class to the appropriate dressing intensity — from the universal foundation of any post-operative dressing, up to active antimicrobial intervention for the dirtiest wounds and established SSI.

Class IV — Dirty / Established SSI — SSI rate >27%. Active antimicrobial wound care: nano-silver primary dressing, alginate for cavity, activated charcoal for malodour. Wound culture, possible debridement, NPWT for large defects. Surveillance reporting triggered.
Class III — Contaminated — SSI rate ~10–17%. Open trauma, gross GI spillage. Silver-impregnated dressing as primary; alginate for any cavity; daily inspection. Lower threshold to escalate to active SSI management.
Class II — Clean-Contaminated — SSI rate ~3–8%. Elective colorectal, C-section, hysterectomy, cholecystectomy. Silicone foam with silver as the prophylactic post-operative dressing has the strongest clinical evidence in this group.
Class I — Clean — SSI rate <2%. Joint replacement, breast, thyroid, hernia. Silicone-bordered foam, hydrocolloid or interactive film all acceptable; choose by exudate level and cosmetic priority. Silver layer optional for higher-risk patient subgroups.
Universal Surgical Bundle — applies to every patient regardless of CDC class. Skin prep, antibiotic prophylaxis, normothermia, normoglycaemia, proper closure technique, primary dressing left undisturbed 48 hours. No dressing compensates for skipping the bundle.
High-Volume Surgical Sites

Where Most Surgical
Wounds Are Placed

A small number of incision sites account for the majority of surgical volume globally. Each anatomical location carries its own dressing requirements — movement, moisture, cosmetic visibility, infection risk, and patient activity all vary by site.

01

Sternotomy & Cardiac

Long midline incision with significant tension from respiration. Dehiscence carries up to 25% mortality. Requires a dressing that conforms to chest movement, manages serosanguinous drainage in the first 5–7 days, and remains in place across multiple inspections. Silicone foam with silver is standard of care in many cardiothoracic units.

02

Upper Abdomen

Subcostal or midline incisions for cholecystectomy, gastric and hepatobiliary surgery. CDC Class II in most cases. The site moves with respiration and abdominal binding — silicone-bordered foam provides atraumatic edge adherence while accommodating motion. Watch for early SSI signs in obese patients.

03

Lower Abdomen (Pfannenstiel)

The classic C-section and gynaecological incision. Sits in a high-moisture, friction-prone area, often beneath an overhanging panniculus in higher-BMI patients. Silicone foam with silver shows clinical evidence in maintaining low SSI rates in this population; thin profile is comfortable under clothing during infant care.

04

Hip & Knee Joint Replacement

CDC Class I, but SSI consequences catastrophic — prosthesis loss, multi-month antibiotic courses, revision surgery. Patient is mobile and undergoing physiotherapy from day one. The dressing must handle large surgical incisions, tolerate movement, and ideally remain in place for 5–7 days to minimise disturbance.

05

Vascular Access Sites

Groin (femoral), wrist (radial) and other catheter access sites. Risk of haematoma, pseudoaneurysm and local infection. Patients are often elderly, on antiplatelet therapy. Compact, conformable, possibly antimicrobial dressing required; foam or thin hydrocolloid depending on exudate.

06

Cosmetic-Visible Sites

Face, neck, breast, hand. Patient and surgeon priorities include final scar appearance. Ultra-thin hydrocolloid for low-exudate, low-profile, near-invisible cover; transition to silicone scar therapy at the appropriate stage (see Scar Management).

Special Surgical Populations

Adjusting Dressing Choice
by Patient Profile

Two patients having the same operation may need different dressings — because risk factors, anatomy and post-discharge environment differ. These six populations cover most of the variation procurement teams need to plan for.

◐

High-BMI Patients

SSI rates double in obese patients undergoing abdominal surgery. Subcutaneous fat impairs perfusion at the incision; skin folds trap moisture above the wound. Silver-impregnated silicone foam with extended wear time is the evidence-supported choice. Specifically validated in high-BMI C-section cohorts.

◑

Diabetic Patients

Impaired wound healing + immune compromise + microvascular disease. Glycaemic control during the perioperative period is the single most important modifiable factor. Antimicrobial dressing prophylactically; monitor closely for delayed SSI presentation, which is common in this group.

◒

Immunosuppressed / Oncology

Chemotherapy, biologics, transplant immunosuppression, corticosteroid use — all delay healing and elevate infection risk. Soft silicone contact layer or fully non-adhesive foam to avoid medical adhesive-related skin injury (MARSI) on fragile skin. Lower threshold for antimicrobial escalation.

◓

Day-Case / Ambulatory Surgery

Patient is discharged within hours and self-monitors at home. Dressing must be waterproof, comfortable, low-profile and remain in place for the full healing window without intervention. Silicone foam with extended wear time or ultra-thin hydrocolloid for low-exudate wounds. Patient education on SSI signs is essential.

◔

Paediatric Surgical Patients

Smaller incisions, thinner skin barrier, higher cosmetic priority for parents. Atraumatic silicone contact essential — avoid any adhesive that strips epidermis on removal. Hydrocolloid for low-exudate wounds; soft silicone foam for larger or higher-exudate sites.

◕

Elderly & Frail

Multiple comorbidities, fragile skin, possible cognitive impairment affecting self-care. Often discharged to nursing homes or home with family caregivers. Easy-to-apply, comfortable dressings with long wear time reduce burden on community nurses. Watch for overlap with pressure injury risk in immobile post-op patients (see Pressure Injury Care).
