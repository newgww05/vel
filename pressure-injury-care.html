Pressure Injury Care
NPIAP-Aligned Strategy

A pressure injury is local damage to skin and underlying tissue caused by sustained pressure — often combined with shear or friction — over a bony prominence. It is preventable, but once established, every stage demands a fundamentally different dressing strategy. This guide aligns NPIAP/EPUAP staging from Stage I to IV (plus Deep Tissue Pressure Injury and Unstageable lesions) with the right material science, so distributors, hospital procurement and long-term-care providers can match the dressing to the wound.

How Pressure Injuries
Form, and How to Intervene

Pressure injuries are not "skin problems" — they are mechanical and ischemic injuries to deep tissue that surface upward. Once formed, they demand stage-specific intervention: from offloading pressure and restoring microclimate balance to managing exudate, debriding necrosis and controlling bacterial bioburden. Understanding the underlying mechanisms is the foundation of every effective dressing decision.

How Pressure Injuries Form
⬡

Sustained Pressure · Capillary Ischemia

When external pressure on tissue exceeds capillary closing pressure (around 32 mmHg) for an extended period, local microcirculation collapses, oxygen delivery is blocked, and cells progress from reversible hypoxia to irreversible necrosis. This is why pressure injuries cluster at bony prominences — the sacrum, heels, ischial tuberosities and occiput — where soft tissue is thinnest and pressure concentration is highest.

◈

Shear & Friction · Deep Plane Damage

When a patient slides down in bed or a chair, the skin stays where the surface grips it, while underlying tissue moves with gravity. The resulting tangential forces tear blood vessels and deep tissue planes — explaining why some injuries surface as a small skin opening that hides a much larger deep cavity below. Friction additionally strips the stratum corneum, weakening the skin's first line of defence.

◇

Microclimate Imbalance · Barrier Failure

Trapped moisture (sweat, incontinence, exudate) combined with heat raises skin pH and softens the corneum. Hyper-hydrated skin tolerates 5× less friction than dry skin and is significantly more vulnerable to maceration and infection. Maintaining an optimal moisture and temperature microclimate at the skin–support surface interface is therefore as important as redistributing pressure itself.

NPIAP / EPUAP Staging

The Six Stages of
Pressure Injury

The 2016 NPIAP staging system is the international clinical standard. Each stage describes a distinct depth of tissue damage and demands a different dressing strategy — getting the stage right is the precondition for getting the dressing right.

Stage IIntact skin with non-blanchable erythema. A localized area of unblanchable redness, usually over a bony prominence. The skin is unbroken; pain, firmness or temperature change may precede the colour change.
Stage IIPartial-thickness skin loss with exposed dermis. Presents as a shallow open ulcer with a red-pink wound bed, or as an intact / ruptured serum-filled blister. No granulation tissue, slough or eschar.
Stage IIIFull-thickness skin loss; subcutaneous fat visible. Bone, tendon and muscle are not exposed. Slough and undermining or tunnelling may be present. Depth varies by anatomical location.
Stage IVFull-thickness skin and tissue loss; bone, tendon or muscle exposed. Slough and eschar are often present along with extensive undermining and tunnelling. High risk of osteomyelitis.
UnstageableObscured full-thickness loss. The base of the ulcer is covered by slough or eschar so the true depth cannot be determined. Stage III or IV will be confirmed only after debridement.
DTPIDeep Tissue Pressure Injury — intact or non-intact skin with persistent non-blanchable deep red, maroon or purple discoloration. Often evolves rapidly to reveal the true depth of injury below.
Care Elements

The TIME-H Framework
for Pressure Injuries

Built on the internationally adopted TIME wound bed preparation framework (extended to TIME-H), this five-step approach pairs every clinical priority with a matching dressing material.

T

Tissue · Debride Necrotic Tissue

Slough and eschar must be removed for healing to proceed. Autolytic debridement uses moisture-retentive dressings (hydrogels, hydrocolloids); enzymatic and sharp debridement may also be required.

I

Infection · Reduce Bioburden

Critical colonization, biofilm and frank infection stall healing. Antimicrobial dressings — ionic silver, nano-silver, activated charcoal — reduce bacterial load and target multi-drug-resistant organisms including MRSA.

M

Moisture · Balance Exudate

Too wet macerates; too dry desiccates. Match absorbency to exudate level — alginates and superabsorbents for heavy, foam for moderate, hydrocolloid or film for light.

E

Edge · Support Epithelial Advance

Once the wound bed is clean and balanced, undermined or rolled edges block re-epithelialisation. Soft silicone contact layers protect new granulation tissue and accelerate edge advance.

H

Healing Environment · Offload & Protect

No dressing replaces pressure offloading. Repositioning every 2 hours, appropriate support surfaces, and prophylactic silicone foam over high-risk bony prominences are non-negotiable foundations.

Stage-by-Stage Dressing Strategy

From Stage I Redness
to Stage IV Cavity

I

Stage I · Intact Erythema

Offload pressure immediately. Cover with prophylactic silicone foam or thin hydrocolloid to reduce friction and microclimate disturbance. No occlusive necessity yet — observation is the priority.

II

Stage II · Partial-Thickness

Maintain moist healing for re-epithelialisation. Hydrocolloid (super-thin) for low exudate; silicone-bordered foam if exudate is moderate or peri-wound skin is fragile. Change every 3–5 days.

III

Stage III · Full-Thickness

Manage moderate-to-heavy exudate, support autolytic debridement, guard against infection. Foam with silver as the workhorse; layer calcium alginate when exudate is heavy or cavity present.

IV

Stage IV · Bone Exposed

Pack cavities and tunnels with alginate rope. Consider nano-silver antimicrobial when bioburden is high or osteomyelitis risk is present. Activated charcoal addresses malodour. Multidisciplinary referral.

U

Unstageable · Eschar Covered

Debride before staging. Hydrogel softens dry eschar for autolytic debridement; alginate handles moist slough. Stage and re-plan once the wound bed is visualised.

DTI

DTPI · Deep Discoloration

Offload aggressively and observe — DTPI often evolves within days. Avoid opaque dressings that hide colour change. Transparent film or thin silicone over the area; reassess every shift.

Prevention · The First Line of Defence

Why Prevention Costs
Less Than Treatment

Once a Stage III or IV pressure injury develops, treatment can run several months and tens of thousands of dollars per patient. A consistent prevention bundle — risk screening, repositioning, surface choice, skin care, nutrition and prophylactic dressings — can avoid most injuries before they form. This is where wound dressings step into a proactive role rather than a reactive one.

Red flags — escalate clinically if
  • Non-blanchable erythema persists over a bony prominence for more than 30 minutes after pressure relief
  • The wound bed exposes muscle, tendon or bone, or undermining is felt with a sterile probe
  • Surrounding skin becomes warm, indurated, increasingly painful or shows spreading erythema (cellulitis suspect)
  • Malodour, copious purulent exudate or systemic signs of infection (fever, raised WBC)
  • Risk screening on admission — Braden, Norton or Waterlow scoring within 8 hours of admission and at every transfer of care.
  • Reposition every 2 hours for bed-bound patients, every 15 minutes for those in chairs. Use the 30° tilt rather than direct lateral.
  • Match the support surface to the risk — reactive foam mattresses for at-risk; alternating-pressure systems for high-risk; specialist surfaces for Stage III–IV.
  • Apply prophylactic silicone foam over the sacrum, heels and other high-risk bony prominences — clinical evidence supports a meaningful reduction in incidence.
  • Manage incontinence and moisture aggressively — skin barrier films and absorbent products to protect against moisture-associated skin damage (MASD).
  • Nutrition assessment — adequate protein (1.2–1.5 g/kg/day for at-risk patients), hydration, and micronutrient support (vitamin C, zinc) for tissue resilience.
  • Heel offloading — float heels off the bed entirely with pillows or wedges; the heel has minimal subcutaneous tissue and tolerates pressure poorly.
Dressing × Stage

The Pressure Injury
Dressing Pyramid

The deeper the injury, the more specialised the dressing. This pyramid maps each NPIAP stage to its primary dressing strategy — the foundation is universal prevention, the apex is reserved for the most severe cases.

Stage IV — bone, tendon or muscle exposed. Nano-silver for high bioburden, calcium alginate rope for cavity packing, activated charcoal for malodour, NPWT for selected cases. Multidisciplinary care.
Stage III — full-thickness, subcutaneous fat visible. Silicone foam with silver is the clinical workhorse; calcium alginate for heavy exudate or cavity; autolytic debridement supported by occlusive moisture balance.
Stage II — partial-thickness with exposed dermis. Super-thin hydrocolloid for low exudate, silicone-bordered foam if exudate moderate or peri-wound skin fragile. 3–5 day wear time.
Stage I + At-Risk — intact erythema or high-risk skin. Prophylactic silicone foam dressings over sacrum and heels reduce incidence (clinical evidence). Transparent film for observation only.
Universal Prevention — applies to every at-risk patient regardless of stage. Risk screening, 2-hour repositioning, appropriate support surfaces, moisture management, nutrition. No dressing can substitute for these.
High-Risk Anatomy

Where Pressure
Injuries Form

Pressure injuries cluster at bony prominences — areas where soft tissue is thin and external pressure concentrates. The seven sites below account for over 90% of all pressure injuries in clinical practice. The position the patient most often occupies (supine, lateral, seated) determines which sites are most at risk.

01

Sacrum & Coccyx

The single most common site (~30% of all pressure injuries). Thin tissue cover, frequent friction during bed mobility, and exposure to incontinence-related moisture make this area particularly vulnerable. Prophylactic silicone foam over the sacrum is one of the most evidence-supported preventive interventions.

02

Heels

Second most common (~20%). The calcaneus has minimal subcutaneous tissue and tolerates pressure poorly. Float heels off the mattress entirely with pillows or commercial offloading boots — pressure-redistribution mattresses alone do not protect the heel.

03

Ischial Tuberosity

Critical for wheelchair-bound and seated patients. Sitting concentrates body weight onto a small bony area; injuries here can extend deep into the gluteal muscles. Cushion selection and 15-minute repositioning intervals are essential.

04

Greater Trochanter

The hip bone projection. Critical for patients lying on their sides — use the 30° tilt (rather than full lateral) to reduce direct pressure on this prominence.

05

Occiput & Scapula

Often overlooked. The occiput is the most common pressure injury site in paediatric and neonatal patients (the head is proportionally larger). Scapular injuries occur with prolonged supine positioning, particularly in ventilated patients.

06

Medical Device-Related (MDR)

An increasingly recognised category — pressure injuries caused by tubes, masks, splints, restraints or compression devices. Often missed because they occur at non-typical anatomical sites. Inspect skin under every device, every shift.

Special Populations

Tailoring Care for
Different Patients

Pressure injury risk and dressing requirements vary substantially across patient populations. The same wound can demand different products depending on who the patient is.

◐

Geriatric Patients

Skin thickness decreases ~20% per decade after 60. The dermis becomes papyraceous, capillaries fragile, healing capacity reduced. Avoid all aggressive adhesives — silicone borders or fully non-adhesive foam with secondary fixation are first-line choices. Watch for medical adhesive-related skin injury (MARSI) at every change.

◑

Diabetic Patients

Microvascular disease + peripheral neuropathy = high risk + delayed recognition. Diabetic foot pressure injuries can progress silently to deep tissue damage. Aggressive infection control — low threshold for nano-silver antimicrobial dressings; consider MRSA and biofilm at every assessment.

◒

Spinal Cord Injury

Lifelong sensory loss below the lesion means no pain warning of developing injury. Ischial tuberosity injuries from prolonged sitting are the highest cause of unplanned hospitalisation in this population. Custom seating, scheduled pressure relief manoeuvres every 15 minutes, and rigorous home dressing protocols.

◓

ICU & Ventilated Patients

Vasopressor-induced tissue hypoperfusion + immobility + medical devices = rapidly developing injury, often DTPI. Devices (ETT, NG tube, NIV mask) cause MDR pressure injuries. Prophylactic silicone dressings under high-risk devices, daily 4-eye skin inspection.

◔

Paediatric & Neonatal

The head is proportionally larger — occipital pressure injuries dominate. Skin barrier is thin in preterm neonates, with high MARSI risk. Specialised paediatric silicone products, micro-adjustments to head position every 2–4 hours.

◕

Palliative Care

Goals shift from healing to comfort and dignity. Kennedy Terminal Ulcers may develop hours to days before death and are not preventable. Activated charcoal addresses malodour; gentle non-adherent dressings prioritise pain-free changes over absorbency optimisation.
