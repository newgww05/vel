After the Subcuticular Stitch — The Reinforced Surface Layer the Surgeon Lays Last.

Modern surgical skin closure rarely ends with the dermal suture itself. After the surgeon completes a subcuticular stitch — a continuous absorbable suture running below the skin surface that approximates the deeper dermal layer — a strip is applied across the closed incision line as the surface-layer closure. The strip distributes tension across a broader surface than the suture alone, supporting the apposed edges through the early remodelling phase when collagen cross-linking is still building. This two-layer approach has become the standard skin-closure technique in total knee arthroplasty (TKA), Caesarean section, breast surgery, abdominal procedures, and laparoscopic port-site closure — combining the strength of an absorbable subcuticular suture with the gentle, broad-area tension support of a surface strip.

The Velcura Skin Closure Strip is built specifically for this surgical role. The backing is reinforced — either a high-strength non-woven or a thread-reinforced composite — providing significantly higher tensile strength than a primary wound-closure strip designed for minor lacerations. The pressure-sensitive acrylic adhesive is high-tack but hypoallergenic and latex-free, securing the strip across the incision through the immediate post-operative period and lifting cleanly at the scheduled removal time. Available widths from 3 mm for delicate facial incisions through 25 mm for major abdominal or arthroplasty wounds. Distinct from a primary wound closure strip — which is designed to close small, low-tension lacerations on its own without any deeper suture beneath — the Skin Closure Strip is positioned in the operative workflow as the layer that goes on after the surgeon has already completed the dermal closure.

Surface Layer After Subcuticular Suture

The defining role of a surgical skin-closure strip. The surgeon completes the dermal closure with an absorbable subcuticular stitch, then applies the strip across the apposed incision line. The strip is not the primary closure — it works with the suture, distributing tension across a broader area than the suture alone, supporting the apposed edges through the critical early remodelling phase.

Reinforced Backing — Built for the OR

Reinforced non-woven or thread-reinforced backing provides significantly higher tensile strength than primary wound-closure strips designed for minor lacerations. Distributes tension across the full incision line for hours and days post-operatively, resisting the cyclic loading of patient movement, breathing and limb function on incisions that may run 10–25 cm.

Documented Clinical Use in OR Specialties

Routine in the standard skin-closure technique for total knee arthroplasty (TKA) and other joint arthroplasty, Caesarean section, abdominal surgery, breast surgery, laparoscopic port-site closure, and paediatric surgical closures where suture removal would be distressing — across the most common modern surgical disciplines.

Cautions
  • Not a primary closure device. The Skin Closure Strip is positioned as the surface layer over a subcuticular suture or as reinforcement after suture/staple removal — wound edges must be properly apposed by the deeper dermal closure (subcuticular suture or equivalent) before strip application. For wounds that do not require a deeper dermal closure, use Wound Closure Strips.
  • Apply only to clean, dry, antiseptic-prepared skin around the closed incision — allow skin antiseptic to dry fully before application, otherwise strip adhesion is compromised.
  • Apply strips perpendicular to the incision line with even spacing — do not stretch the strip during application; pre-tension trapped under the adhesive can create edge lift and asymmetric tension across the closure.
  • Not indicated for infected wounds, wounds with non-viable edges, or wounds under significant unresolved tension — these require surgical re-evaluation, not strip closure.
  • Remove slowly and parallel to the skin surface at scheduled removal time to minimise medical adhesive-related skin injury (MARSI) — particularly important in geriatric and dermatology patients.
  • Single-use sterile device — do not resterilize. Discard immediately if packaging integrity is compromised or beyond expiry date.
