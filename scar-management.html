Why Scars Form
and How to Intervene

A scar is the skin's overcompensation to mechanical tension. When a wound heals under excessive stretch, fibroblasts are mechanically activated to overproduce collagen — the root of raised, hypertrophic scarring. Understanding this is where effective, evidence-based scar management begins.

How Scars Form
⬡

Mechanical Tension · Force Signals Activated

Under sustained stretch, fibroblasts are activated through mechanotransduction pathways such as the Piezo1 ion channel, YAP and FAK, shifting toward a fibro-proliferative phenotype. This is why wounds in high-tension areas — the lower abdomen, joints and chest — are more prone to hypertrophic scarring.

◈

Collagen Overdeposition · Abnormal Remodeling

Driven by both inflammation and mechanical stimulation, collagen synthesis and breakdown fall out of balance and accumulate excessively, leaving the scar raised and firm. Hypertrophic scars stay within the original wound border, while keloids keep expanding into surrounding healthy skin.

◇

Barrier Damage · Elevated Water Loss

A new scar's surface barrier is incomplete, and the resulting rise in transepidermal water loss (TEWL) signals fibroblasts to "keep repairing," further driving proliferation. Restoring an adequately moist barrier environment is a key step in suppressing overgrowth.

Scar Types

Common Scar Types

Different scar types vary in appearance, symptoms and management strategy. Accurately identifying the scar type is the first step toward a personalized care plan and the right dressing choice.

ImmatureEarly after wound closure — red, slightly raised, sometimes itchy; the golden window for silicone and tension intervention.
MatureAfter months to two years of remodeling, it flattens, softens and fades toward normal skin.
HypertrophicRed, firm, raised, itchy and painful, but stays within the original wound border; closely linked to mechanical tension.
KeloidGrows beyond the original wound and keeps expanding; common on the chest, shoulders and earlobes, and stubborn to treat.
AtrophicA depressed scar sitting below the surrounding skin, often seen after acne and chickenpox.
ContractureCommon after burns — tightened tissue can cross joints and restrict movement.
Care Elements

Five Elements of Scar Management

Centered on "tension offloading + barrier repair," systematically covering every stage that influences scar maturation.

1

Tension Regulation

Tension-offloading tape or fixation strips draw the skin on both sides of the incision inward, reducing mechanical tension — the key to preventing hypertrophic scars.

2

Silicone Therapy

Silicone gel or sheets work through occlusive hydration, signaling fibroblasts to slow collagen production while softening and flattening the scar.

3

Barrier Hydration

Maintaining adequate moisture and reducing transepidermal water loss restores an intact skin barrier and suppresses overgrowth signals.

4

Manual Massage

Once the wound is fully closed, professional circular massage releases deep tissue adhesions, improves circulation and eases tightness.

5

Sun Protection

Melanocytes in a new scar overreact to UV; strict sun protection prevents long-term pigmentation.

Standard Care Pathway

Six Stages from Wound Closure to Scar Maturity

1

Wound Closure

Keep clean, avoid infection and premature tension, and wait for full epithelialization (about 2–3 weeks post-op).

2

Start Tension Offloading

Once epithelialized, apply tension-offloading tape to relieve mechanical tension and prevent overgrowth at its source.

3

Introduce Silicone

Wear sheets or gel at least 12 hours a day for occlusive hydration that curbs excess collagen deposition.

4

Manual Massage

Circular massage releases deep adhesions, improves circulation and eases tightness and discomfort.

5

Sun Protection

Protect strictly from the sun to avoid pigmentation, and keep it up until the scar matures (at least 3 months).

6

Review & Referral

Seek medical care promptly for stubborn scars, moving on to targeted options such as injections or laser therapy.

C-Section Scars · Home Care

C-Section Scar
Care in Seven Points

A C-section incision sits on the lower abdomen — high in tension and constantly rubbed by clothing — making it a classic high-risk scar site. Care is not only about fading the mark, but about easing post-op abdominal tightness and preventing deep adhesions, so mothers can look after their own bodies with ease while caring for a newborn.

See a doctor promptly if you notice
  • Redness, discharge, fever or persistent pain — possible infection
  • The scar rapidly raising, hardening or spreading beyond the original wound
  • Noticeable pulling, tightness or restricted movement in the abdomen
  • Worsening itching or stinging that disrupts daily life
  • Begin silicone and tension care only after the wound is fully closed (about 2–3 weeks post-op, confirmed by your doctor).
  • Wear silicone sheets at least 12 hours a day, extending coverage where possible, and clean daily.
  • Because the scar sits in a clothing-friction zone, silicone sheets usually suit the C-section site better than gel and stay in place more securely.
  • Pairing with medical tension-offloading tape to relieve lower-abdominal tension is the single most important step in preventing hypertrophic scars.
  • Once healed, use your fingertips for a few minutes of circular massage daily to release adhesions and ease tightness.
  • Protect the scar strictly from the sun when outdoors to avoid long-term pigmentation.
  • Consistency is key: proactive care should usually continue for at least 3 months, across the full scar-maturation period.
