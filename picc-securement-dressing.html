The Window That Kills What It Watches — Continuous CHG Antimicrobial Through a Fully Visible Insertion Site.

For decades the trade-off in catheter securement was binary: a plain transparent IV film let you see the insertion site but offered no antimicrobial protection beneath; a gauze-and-CHG-disc dressing offered antimicrobial protection but obscured the site, requiring dressing removal for every visual assessment. The PICC Securement Dressing collapses that trade-off into a single product. A chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG) gel pad sits directly over the catheter insertion site, delivering continuous broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity for up to seven days. Both the polyurethane film and the CHG gel are transparent — clinicians can inspect the puncture site, suture wing, hub and proximal catheter for early signs of phlebitis, infiltration, dislodgement, erythema or exudate at any time, without removing the dressing and breaking the sterile field.

Clinical evidence supports the CHG-impregnated approach. A multicentre randomised trial in critically ill adults (Timsit et al., Am J Respir Crit Care Med, 2012) reported reduced catheter-related infections with CHG-impregnated transparent dressings. A German prospective study (Scheithauer et al., Infection, 2014) and a French cost-effectiveness analysis (Maunoury et al., PLoS One, 2015) corroborated the clinical and economic benefit. CDC central-line bundle guidance, SHEA strategic recommendations, and INS Infusion Therapy Standards of Practice all recognise CHG-impregnated dressings in central-line care for patients with prolonged central venous access, particularly in ICU and oncology settings. The Velcura PICC Securement Dressing also features a reinforced stabilising border with conforming edge technology that resists edge-lift over multi-day wear, and a keyhole catheter notch that accommodates the catheter lumen for sutureless securement — eliminating the needlestick-injury risk of suture-based securement. Distinct from a plain transparent IV film dressing — which secures the catheter but offers no antimicrobial activity beneath the film — the PICC Securement Dressing combines both functions in a single product.

Integrated CHG Gel Pad · 7-Day Antimicrobial

A chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG) gel pad sits directly over the catheter insertion site, delivering continuous broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity at the skin-catheter interface for up to seven days. The CHG is bonded into a transparent hydrogel matrix — it stays in place at the puncture site, working through every shift, every assessment, every fluid administration.

Transparent · Both Film and Gel

Polyurethane film and CHG gel pad are both fully transparent — clinicians inspect the insertion site, suture wing, hub and proximal catheter for early signs of phlebitis, infiltration, dislodgement, erythema or exudate at any time, without removing the dressing and breaking the sterile field. The decision to change a dressing is driven by clinical assessment, not by needing to remove it to perform one.

Clinical Evidence for CLABSI/CRBSI Reduction

CHG-impregnated transparent dressings are clinically associated with reduced catheter-related bloodstream infections in published research: Timsit 2012 multicentre RCT in critically ill adults (Am J Respir Crit Care Med); Scheithauer 2014 prospective study (Infection); Maunoury 2015 cost-effectiveness analysis (PLoS One). Aligned with CDC, SHEA and INS Infusion Therapy Standards of Practice central-line bundle guidance.

Reinforced Border · Keyhole Catheter Notch

Reinforced stabilising border with conforming edge technology resists edge-lift over multi-day wear — keeping the CHG pad in continuous contact with the insertion site and the catheter in place. The keyhole notch accommodates the catheter lumen for sutureless securement, eliminating the needlestick-injury risk of suture-based catheter securement.

Cautions
  • Contraindicated in patients with known chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG) hypersensitivity. Always confirm allergy history before use; choose a non-antimicrobial transparent IV dressing for CHG-sensitive patients.
  • Use with caution in premature neonates and infants under 2 months of age — neonatal skin is highly permeable and CHG systemic absorption may occur; follow local neonatal protocol and consult the institutional CHG policy.
  • Change dressing per institutional guidelines (typically every 7 days when intact), or sooner if integrity is compromised, soiled, blood-saturated, or if the insertion site requires assessment beyond what visualisation through the dressing permits.
  • Apply only to clean, dry, antiseptic-prepared skin around the catheter insertion site — allow skin antiseptic to dry fully before application, otherwise the CHG gel pad will not maintain optimal contact and the adhesive bond is compromised.
  • Position the CHG gel pad directly over the catheter insertion site — the antimicrobial activity is local; misalignment of the gel pad away from the puncture site reduces protection.
  • Remove slowly and parallel to the skin surface at scheduled change to minimise MARSI — particularly important on the thin skin of geriatric, oncology and long-stay patients with multiple dressing changes at the same site.
  • Single-use sterile device — do not resterilize. Discard immediately if packaging integrity is compromised or beyond expiry date.
